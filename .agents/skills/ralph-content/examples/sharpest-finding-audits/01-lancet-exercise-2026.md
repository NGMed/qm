# Audit: Shan et al. — Lancet Healthy Longevity, April 29, 2026

**Paper:** "Physical activity and biological age measured by DNA methylation clocks: a systematic review and meta-analysis"

**Source:** *Lancet Healthy Longevity*, Article 100835. Open access.

**Audit goal:** Identify the editorial anchor for an NGM LinkedIn post.

## Step 1 — Candidate findings enumerated

Read end-to-end (not just the abstract). Five candidate findings surface:

1. **GrimAge β = -0.09 across 145,465 people.** The abstract headline. The pooled effect size on the most mortality-predictive epigenetic clock when meta-analyzing 7 studies harmonized to MET-min/week.

2. **Hannum and PhenoAge clocks showed NO significant association.** Two of the four major epigenetic clocks examined did not register an exercise signal in the meta-analysis (Hannum β = -0.01 [-0.07 to 0.05]; PhenoAge β = -0.09 [-0.28 to 0.09], CIs cross zero).

3. **Horvath signal driven by a single influential study.** The authors' own caveat in the discussion: in fixed-effects sensitivity analysis, GrimAge held but Horvath did not. The Horvath finding "appears largely driven by a single influential study" (Spartano et al. 2023, FHS, n=2,435).

4. **Occupational physical activity (heavy manual labor) showed NO association with any clock.** Buried in section 3.4 of the results. Structured leisure-time exercise carried the entire signal in the meta-analysis. Pooled and sex-stratified analyses both null for occupational PA.

5. **Funnel-plot asymmetry suggests publication bias** for both Horvath and GrimAge findings, meaning studies finding null results may have been less likely to be published.

## Step 3 — The four "tells" check on the leading candidate

Candidate 4 (occupational vs leisure paradox) applies all four:

- **Everyday-life implication:** Yes. Changes the clinician's response to "but I'm on my feet all day at work."
- **Contradicts conventional wisdom:** Yes. "Movement is movement" is the default assumption a thoughtful patient would hold.
- **"Wait, why?" attached:** Yes. The mechanism is genuinely puzzling — recovery context? structured intensity? psychosocial stress? sleep disruption? Each is a teachable moment for an NGM post.
- **Actionable now:** Yes. Changes how clinicians frame the exercise prescription tomorrow without waiting for further data.

All four tells fire. This is the anchor.

## Step 4 — Anchor selected

**Editorial anchor:** Occupational physical activity showed no association with any biological-age clock. Only structured leisure-time exercise registered. Score: 24/25.

**Was this the abstract headline?** No. The abstract foregrounds GrimAge β = -0.09. The occupational-vs-leisure finding lives in section 3.4 of the results.

**Justification needed?** No. The audit-selected anchor is not the abstract headline.

## Step 5 — How this drives the post

**Hook template (one of the 6):** Industry-Reframe-Plus-Alternative or Tension-Between-Belief-And-Data. Both fit because the finding overturns "movement is movement" with a specific empirical contrast.

**Body cadence:** BJ-adapted (period-heavy declarative, numerical specificity per paragraph, asymmetric honesty embedded).

**The post leads with:** "Why does heavy manual labor not lower biological age, when structured exercise does?" The 145,465-person GrimAge effect size becomes a body bullet, not the lead.

**The post does NOT lead with:** "Exercise lowered GrimAge by β = -0.09 across 145,465 people." (That is the abstract headline. Every other longevity newsletter will lead with that. NGM differentiates by leading with the buried-but-sharp finding.)

## Lesson archived

For meta-analyses and large pooled studies, the abstract typically foregrounds the *primary effect size*. The sharpest finding is often a *contrast within the data* (subgroup analyses, sex-stratified results, occupational vs leisure splits, RCT vs cross-sectional sub-comparisons). Hunt for these contrasts deliberately. They are where NGM's editorial differentiation lives.
