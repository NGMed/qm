# Quality Rubrics

Quality evaluation criteria for all content types. Use these to self-critique and decide when content is ready.

---

## Global Fail Conditions (Apply to ALL Content Types)

Before evaluating any content-specific rubric, check these. If ANY global fail condition is triggered, the content fails regardless of other scores.

### Banned: "Zero Echo" / Dramatic Repetition
**Fails when:** The content states a fact (especially absence of evidence) and then repeats the number or phrase on its own line for dramatic emphasis. Examples: "Zero RCTs exist.\n\nZero." or "Not a single trial.\n\nNot one." This is lazy writing that substitutes theater for analysis. When evidence is absent, move directly to implications, adjacent evidence, or what would need to be true. The absence is one sentence in a paragraph, not a standalone dramatic beat.

### Banned: "Let That Sink In" Pause
**Fails when:** Any variant of "let that sink in," "read that again," "think about that," or "I'll say it again" appears. Respect the reader's intelligence.

## Newsletter Quality Rubric (Every.to 8-Point)

Evaluate each criterion. Content is ready when all 8 pass AND no global fail conditions are triggered.

### 1. Specificity Check
**Question:** Does EVERY significant claim include names, numbers, and timeframes?

**Passes when:**
- Names: Specific people, companies, products (never "experts say" or "a startup")
- Numbers: Exact figures ("$2.5B" not "billions")
- Timeframes: Specific periods ("between early 2025 and fall" not "recently")
- The piece passes the "Ctrl+F test" — searching for "many," "several," "significant," "experts" returns zero vague results

**Fails when:** More than 2 paragraphs lack specific names/numbers

### 2. Evidence Cascade Present
**Question:** Are there 3+ specific examples stacked together for major claims?

**Passes when:**
- Pattern appears: "[Company A] achieved [metric]. Similarly, [Company B] [result]. As did [Company C] with [data]."
- Uses connectors: "similarly," "as did," "including"
- At least one evidence cascade appears for a key claim

**Fails when:** Major claims rely on single examples or generalizations

### 3. Colon Technique Used
**Question:** Does the piece use colon technique to introduce key concepts?

**Passes when:**
- Patterns like "Here's what matters: [insight]" or "The key limitation: [issue]" appear
- Used 3+ times for thesis introduction, framework points, or clarifications
- Strategic use (not just in lists)

**Fails when:** No strategic colon usage or fewer than 3 instances

### 4. Clean Punctuation
**Question:** Is the piece free of em-dashes, using periods, colons, commas, and parentheses instead?

**Allowed alternatives:**
1. Clarification: "The inflammasome (NLRP3 specifically) acts as..."
2. Context/aside: "The writer, now at Pace Capital, explored..."
3. Contrast: "Not the best models. The ones that own infrastructure."

**Passes when:** Zero em-dashes appear. Periods, colons, commas, and parentheses handle all clarification, aside, and contrast functions.

**Fails when:** Any em-dash (—) appears in the content

### 5. One-Sentence Emphasis
**Question:** Are there strategic one-sentence paragraphs at key moments?

**Passes when:**
- 3-6 one-sentence paragraphs appear
- Placed after building context, delivering punchlines
- Examples: "It's pure ruthlessness." "And it's not close." "The timing matters more than the dose."

**Fails when:** No one-sentence paragraphs, or more than 6 (overused), or multiple in a row

### 6. Concrete Over Abstract (with Variety)
**Question:** Does the opening ground readers in something tangible AND avoid the same pattern as recent newsletters?

**Hook types that pass (use a DIFFERENT one each time):**
- Counterintuitive claim: "[Accepted truth]. [Evidence that inverts it]."
- Direct declaration: "[Thesis in one sentence. No warm-up.]"
- Statistic that reframes: "[Striking number]. [Context]. [Pivot.]"
- Question that reveals: "[Question exposing a hidden assumption]."
- Historical parallel: "[Specific past event]. [Draw the parallel.]"
- Specific case/vignette: "Dr. Sarah Chen at Stanford saw..."
- Tension between two truths: "[Truth A]. [Truth B]. [Name the gap.]"
- Pattern recognition: "I've spent [timeframe] [doing X], watching [pattern]."
- Paradox: "The standard approach assumes... But..."
- Metaphor with sensory detail

**Fails when:**
- Opening with "In today's rapidly evolving..." or "Longevity medicine is..."
- First paragraph lacks concrete details (names, places, numbers, sensory info)
- **Opening uses the same construction (e.g., "A [age]-year-old [profession]" clinical vignette) as 2+ other recent newsletters. The specific-case/vignette pattern should appear in no more than 1 in 5 newsletters.**

### 7. Voice Authenticity
**Question:** Does it match Every.to voice principles?

**Passes when:**
- Intellectually rigorous yet accessible
- Confident assertions backed by evidence
- Personal without self-indulgence
- Uses characteristic phrases: "Just to be real with you," "Here's what that means"
- No hedging with "might" when "will" is meant

**Fails when:** Feels generic, corporate, or excessively hedged

### 8. Forward-Looking Conclusion
**Question:** Does the conclusion point forward, not summarize?

**Patterns that pass:**
- "And the next [timeframe] will determine [stakes]"
- "If you [condition], you need to [action]"

**Fails when:**
- "In conclusion, we've covered..." or "To summarize the key points..."
- Ending discusses what was said instead of implications

### 9. Huberman Approachability Check (MANDATORY for newsletters)

**Question:** Can a time-pressed clinician skim this piece and still absorb the core insight without needing to read every sentence?

Evaluate each sub-criterion. All six must pass. See `context/every-voice-patterns.md` → "Huberman Accessibility Patterns" for rule definitions.

- **9a — short_paragraphs**: Paragraphs are 1–3 sentences. Fewer than 20% of paragraphs run 4+ sentences. Run the test: count every paragraph. If >20% are 4-sentence or longer, **fail**.
- **9b — analogy_before_mechanism**: Every molecular detail, pathway, or unfamiliar term is preceded by a plain-English analogy ("Think of it like…", "Picture this:…", "Imagine…"). Fails if any mechanism paragraph opens with a protein name, acronym, or pathway term without a preceding analogy.
- **9c — signpost_why_this_matters**: 3–5 signposts appear between major sections using phrases like "Here's why this matters for practice:", "The clinical implication:", "What this changes:". Fails if transitions between sections are bare or if fewer than 3 signposts exist.
- **9d — one_sentence_emphasis_expanded**: 4–8 one-sentence paragraphs, never two in a row. (This is a lift from Every's 3–6 range because Huberman style uses more breathing room.)
- **9e — walk_through_phrasing**: 3–6 active teaching phrases appear ("Let me walk you through this.", "Here's the key idea:", "Let's unpack that.", "Stay with me — this matters."). Fails if fewer than 3 appear.
- **9f — progressive_mechanism_disclosure**: Every section follows hook → analogy → thesis → mechanism → implication. Fails if any section opens with a protein name, pathway acronym, or chemical formula before setting up the mental model.

**Passes when:** All 6 sub-criteria are TRUE. If any fail, rewrite the affected sections — do not ship dense newsletters.

## LinkedIn Quality Rubric (14-Point)

For LinkedIn posts derived from newsletters or standalone.

### Hook Criteria (3 points)
1. **pattern_interrupt**: Does the first line create a pattern interrupt? TRUE if it stops the scroll with something unexpected.
2. **hook_under_150_chars**: Is the hook under 150 characters? TRUE if the opening line is concise and punchy.
3. **creates_curiosity**: Does the hook create genuine curiosity while signaling the topic? TRUE if the reader knows what the post is about AND wants to keep scrolling. Fails if the hook is too vague ("told me something I can't stop thinking about") or gives away the full insight upfront. See "Curiosity Architecture" in voice patterns.

### Curiosity-Driven Hook Architecture Check (MANDATORY — applies to LinkedIn, newsletter opens, lead magnet titles, Instagram first-3-seconds)

These criteria reflect the Bryan-Johnson-pattern-derived hook architecture documented in `every-voice-patterns.md` → "Curiosity-Driven Hook Architecture." All four must pass.

- **3a — results_first_in_hook**: The hook leads with a specific outcome, finding, or contradiction, not with methodology, citations, or context. Fails if the first sentence is "Recent research has explored..." or "There is growing interest in..." or "Studies have shown..." Passes if the first sentence delivers the headline finding before the audience expects it.
- **3b — specificity_in_hook**: The hook contains at least one specific number, named study, exact percentage, or measurable comparison. Fails if the hook reads as if it could apply to any topic. "44 studies, 145,465 people" passes; "the largest study ever" fails.
- **3c — no_hedging_in_hook**: The hook avoids hedging language (may, might, could, potentially, in some cases, appears to, tends to). Hedging belongs in the body, not the hook. The hook makes the strongest defensible claim.
- **3d — uses_curiosity_template**: The hook fits one of the six curiosity-driven templates (Results-Just-Came-In, Superlative-Plus-Contradiction, Specific-Metric-With-Population-Reference, Industry-Reframe-Plus-Alternative, Unexpected-Finding-With-Implication, Tension-Between-Belief-And-Data) OR the writer can articulate why a different structure better fits the topic. Fails if the hook is a listicle opener, an academic throat-clearer, or a generic health claim.

**Self-audit before publishing:** Apply the test from `every-voice-patterns.md`: "If I removed the rest of the post, would the hook alone make a knowledgeable reader feel they learned something specific?" If no, the hook fails 3a, 3b, or both. Rewrite.

### Bryan-Johnson-Adapted Voice Check (MANDATORY for LinkedIn — applies to body, not just hook)

These criteria reflect the BJ-adapted body voice documented in `every-voice-patterns.md` → "Bryan-Johnson-Adapted Voice (DEFAULT for NGM LinkedIn body)." Skip only if `--voice` explicitly overrides to a non-compatible voice (klosterman). All six must pass for default LinkedIn posts.

- **3e — period_heavy_cadence**: Average sentence length is 10–18 words. Fewer than 20% of sentences exceed 25 words. Periods do the connective work where most writers use semicolons, conjunctions with commas, or em-dashes. Run the test: count words per sentence, then tally the >25-word sentences as a percentage.
- **3f — numerical_specificity_per_paragraph**: Every paragraph in the body contains at least one specific number, named study, or measurable comparison. The Ctrl+F test for "many," "several," "significant," "studies suggest," "research shows" returns zero hits unless followed by a specific n or citation.
- **3g — confident_not_hedged**: The post avoids "may," "might," "could," "potentially," "in some cases," "appears to," "tends to" except where the underlying evidence forces the hedge. Each instance must survive a "what does this hedge actually do?" challenge — if it could be removed without changing the meaning, remove it.
- **3h — asymmetric_honesty_present**: The body acknowledges at least one limitation, null result, or open question. The acknowledgment is embedded in the analysis (not appended as a closing CYA disclaimer). Examples that pass: "Hannum and PhenoAge showed no signal," "Only 3 of 44 studies were RCTs," "Funnel-plot asymmetry suggests publication bias."
- **3i — imperative_close**: The final line is a 2–6 word clinical instruction or calibration directive. Examples that pass: "Prescribe the exercise. Calibrate the language." / "Write the prescription." / "Bring your skepticism." Fails if the post ends with a soft summary or a hedged forward-looking statement.
- **3j — no_off_brand_patterns**: Zero personal n=1 framing of Anant's biomarkers; zero "Don't Die" / civilizational-scale stakes; zero supplement-stack disclosures; zero listicle structures as the spine of the argument; zero aggressive contrarianism untethered to specific data.

**Override:** When `--voice` is set explicitly (klosterman, dan_shipper, daily_stoic, etc.), criteria 3e and 3f may be relaxed for that voice's natural cadence. Criteria 3g, 3h, 3i, 3j still apply across all voices — they are NGM editorial standards, not BJ-specific.

### Sharpest-Finding Anchor Check (MANDATORY for any post anchored to a specific paper)

This criterion enforces the editorial-angle audit from `SKILL.md` Phase 1.6 and `every-voice-patterns.md` → "The Sharpest-Finding Heuristic." Skip only for evergreen pieces not anchored to a specific source.

- **3k — anchor_passes_sharpness_audit**: The editorial anchor of the post is the candidate finding selected by Phase 1.6's 5-criterion sharpness audit, and the audit itself is recorded in the JSON pipeline metadata under `editorialAngleAudit`. Specifically:
  - The metadata MUST include the enumeration of 3–5 candidate findings from the source
  - Each candidate MUST have a recorded score on all 5 criteria (counter-intuitive, practical, mechanism, differentiation, specificity) plus the total
  - The selected anchor MUST match the highest-scoring candidate
  - If the chosen anchor matches the paper's abstract headline, a one-sentence justification MUST be recorded under `editorialAngleAudit.abstractHeadlineJustification`
  - The post's actual anchor (the finding the hook leads with) MUST match the audit-selected anchor
  - **Fails when:** the audit metadata is absent, incomplete, or the post anchors on a finding other than the highest-scoring candidate (without a justification block)

**Editorial standard:** A reader who has already read the paper's abstract should learn something new from the NGM post. If the central finding of the post matches the abstract's stated conclusion *and* the audit was not justified, the post fails this criterion regardless of how well-written the body is.

### Structure Criteria (4 points)
4. **has_clear_thesis**: Is there a clear, specific thesis? TRUE if the main argument is obvious within first 3 lines.
5. **uses_line_breaks**: Are line breaks used effectively? TRUE if single sentences or short phrases have their own lines.
6. **avoids_wall_of_text**: No paragraphs longer than 3 sentences? TRUE if all paragraphs are digestible.
7. **follows_hook_expand_close**: Does it follow Hook → Expand → Close structure?

### Content Criteria (4 points)
8. **has_specific_numbers**: Does it include specific numbers or data points? TRUE if at least one concrete number appears.
9. **avoids_jargon**: Is it free of unexplained jargon? TRUE if all terms are accessible.
10. **has_original_insight**: Is there a non-obvious insight? TRUE if it says something readers haven't heard before. For tool/tech evaluations: security stats and vulnerability counts are the *obvious* angle. Practitioner experience (bugginess, daily friction, operational burden, who's hyping it and why) is where original insight lives. See "Technology Evaluation Lens" in voice patterns.
11. **stays_focused**: Does it focus on one idea? TRUE if not trying to cover multiple topics.

### Engagement Criteria (3 points)
12. **has_subtle_cta**: Is there a call-to-action that feels like a narrative resolution, not a bolted-on promotion? TRUE if the CTA resolves the curiosity gap the post built (e.g., "I asked him to walk through the full system. He's joining us Friday."). Fails if it reads like an ad ("Register for our webinar! Link in comments."). See "Curiosity Architecture" in voice patterns.
13. **paragraphs_punchy**: Are paragraphs brief (2-3 sentences max each)?
14. **intellectually_honest**: Does it acknowledge uncertainty, trade-offs, or complexity where appropriate?

**Passes when:** At least 12 of 14 criteria are TRUE.

### Podcast Roundup LinkedIn Teaser (Additional Rules)

When the LinkedIn post promotes a podcast roundup, these additional rules apply on top of the 14-point rubric:

1. **preserves_curiosity**: The post must NOT deliver the analysis. It teases the *shape* of insights without completing them. Think movie trailer, not executive summary. TRUE if every insight mentioned creates an open loop the reader can only close by getting the full issue.
2. **open_loop_teases**: Include 2-3 specific teases that name what was found but withhold the mechanism or explanation. Each tease should follow the pattern: "[Surprising claim] — and it has nothing to do with [the obvious explanation]."
   - Good: "There's a reason exercise stops protecting the brain as you age — and it has nothing to do with fitness."
   - Bad: "Exercise triggers GPLD1 release from the liver, which replicates cognitive benefits. But the pathway depends on NAD+..."
3. **names_sources**: Reference specific podcast hosts and guests to establish credibility, but do NOT summarize their episodes.
4. **scope_signal**: Give the reader a sense of scale ("7 findings, 5 quick hits, one editorial thesis") so they know what they're getting.
5. **under_280_words**: Roundup teasers should be tight — 200-280 words max.

## Instagram Script Quality Rubric

For spoken-word video scripts (30-90 seconds).

### Hook (3 points)
1. **stops_scroll**: First 3 seconds grab attention with surprising statement or question
2. **under_10_words**: Hook is 10 words or fewer
3. **creates_open_loop**: Hook creates curiosity that body resolves

### Body (4 points)
4. **spoken_naturally**: Written for voice, not text (contractions, conversational)
5. **single_focus**: One clear idea, no tangents
6. **evidence_included**: At least one specific fact, study, or example
7. **correct_duration**: 30-90 seconds when read at natural pace (~150 words/min)

### Close (3 points)
8. **clear_takeaway**: Viewer knows the one thing to remember
9. **has_cta**: Ends with clear next step (follow, comment, save)
10. **no_abrupt_end**: Feels complete, not cut off

**Passes when:** At least 8 of 10 criteria are TRUE.

## Regulatory Brief Quality Rubric (12-Point)

For FDA committee briefs, safety summaries, and technical regulatory documents.

### Structure (4 points)
1. **header_complete**: Header includes document type, subject identifier, and key metadata (MW, sequence, CAS# for peptides)
2. **three_section_structure**: Document has Safety Data, Regulatory Precedent, and Conclusion sections
3. **print_optimized**: Layout fits on one page (8.5"×11") with 0.75" margins
4. **table_format_used**: Route-specific data or comparisons use tables, not prose

### Safety Data Section (4 points)
5. **clinical_trials_specific**: Each trial includes n, study design, and key findings (not vague summaries)
6. **adverse_events_categorized**: AEs categorized as Common/Uncommon/Rare with specific symptoms
7. **safety_signals_explicit**: Safety signals explicitly categorized as KNOWN, THEORETICAL, or ABSENT
8. **route_considerations**: Different administration routes addressed (IV, SC, oral, etc.)

### Sources & Citations (2 points)
9. **all_sources_reputable**: Zero citations to ecommerce sites, vendors, or non-peer-reviewed sources
10. **citations_accurate**: All author names, years, and journals verified against primary source

### Conclusion (2 points)
11. **evidence_gaps_explicit**: Conclusion explicitly states what evidence is missing or limited
12. **recommendation_conditional**: Recommendation includes conditions or alternatives, not binary approve/reject

**Passes when:** All 12 criteria are TRUE. For regulatory content, there is no partial pass.

### Reasoning Consistency Framework

When creating multiple related regulatory briefs, ensure conclusions are proportional to evidence:

| Evidence Type | Relative Weight | Example |
|---------------|-----------------|---------|
| FDA-approved drugs in same class | High | Setmelanotide for melanocortin peptides |
| Human clinical trial data (direct) | High | n=107 withdrawal study |
| Non-U.S. regulatory approval | Medium | Russian Deltaran registration |
| Preclinical animal data only | Medium-Low | Mouse colitis models |
| Mechanism well-characterized | Medium | PepT1/NF-κB pathway |
| Endogenous origin | Low (safety inference only) | Natural peptide fragment |

**Consistency check:** If Document A has human trial data and Document B only has preclinical + class precedent, both can reach favorable conclusions BUT the reasoning must be explicit:
- Document A: "Direct human evidence supports safety"
- Document B: "Class-level precedent and preclinical data support safety, though direct human evidence is absent"

## Weekly Roundup Quality Rubric (12-Point)

For "This Week in Longevity" bilingual weekly newsletter. Applied to English version; Japanese version is checked separately for translation fidelity.

### Coverage (4 points)
1. **four_beats_covered**: All 4 beats (Science, Medical, Business, Tech/AI) have content? TRUE if every beat has at least one story.
2. **min_stories_per_beat**: Each beat has 2-3 stories? TRUE if no beat has fewer than 2 stories. Maximum 3 per beat to maintain focus.
3. **editors_pick_has_analysis**: Editor's Pick includes extended 150-200 word analysis? TRUE if the analysis goes beyond summary to offer unique perspective, practitioner implications, or contrarian insight.
4. **recency_verified**: All stories are from the target week's date range? TRUE if no story is older than 10 days from the newsletter date. Breaking news from just outside the window is acceptable if explicitly noted.

### Analysis Quality (4 points)
5. **analysis_not_summary**: Each story's analysis explains *why it matters*, not just *what happened*? TRUE if every story has at least one sentence addressing implications, practitioner relevance, or what to watch for. The test: could this analysis only be written by someone who understands longevity medicine, or could any news aggregator produce it?
6. **unique_perspective**: At least 3 stories include original analysis that goes beyond restating the news? TRUE if the author's expertise adds value readers can't get from the press release alone. Examples: connecting a funding round to a clinical trend, noting a study's limitations the press coverage missed, explaining what an FDA action means for clinical practice.
7. **no_link_dump**: No story is reduced to just a headline + link? TRUE if every story has both a summary sentence AND an analysis. A weekly roundup without analysis is just an RSS feed.
8. **concrete_specifics**: Stories include specific numbers, names, dates, and details? TRUE if the majority of stories cite at least one specific data point (dollar amounts, patient counts, dates, percentages).

### Structure (2 points)
9. **quick_hits_present**: Quick Hits section exists with 3-5 brief mentions? TRUE if there are items that didn't warrant full treatment but are worth flagging.
10. **what_to_watch_present**: What to Watch section exists with 1-2 forward-looking items? TRUE if the newsletter ends by pointing readers toward upcoming events, expected results, or emerging trends.

### Voice & Engagement (2 points)
11. **sources_cited**: All stories cite their primary source (journal, publication, company)? TRUE if readers can trace each claim back to an original source.
12. **forward_looking_close**: The newsletter closes by looking ahead, not summarizing what was covered? TRUE if the final section points forward rather than recapping.

**Passes when:** At least 10 of 12 criteria are TRUE, AND criteria 1, 5, and 6 are mandatory (must be TRUE).

### Japanese Version Quality Check (supplementary, not scored)

After the English version passes the 12-point rubric, check the Japanese translation for:

| Criterion | Check |
|-----------|-------|
| **Natural flow** | Does it read like a native Japanese newsletter? Not stilted machine-translation patterns. |
| **Facts preserved** | All factual claims from English version are accurately represented. |
| **Keigo level** | Semi-formal です/ます form. Authoritative but accessible (similar to Nikkei health coverage). |
| **Proper nouns** | Company names, drug names, gene names stay in original English/Latin. |
| **Domain terminology** | Standard Japanese medical terms used (再生医療, セノリティクス, エピジェネティクス, etc.) |
| **Cultural adaptation** | US-specific context (FDA, etc.) gets brief explanation where needed for Japanese readers. |
| **No raw English** | No untranslated English sentences in body text (proper nouns excepted). |

## Podcast Roundup Quality Rubric (10-Point)

For biweekly podcast roundup newsletters enriched with KB insights.

### Findings Quality (4 points)
1. **findings_are_specific**: Each finding leads with a specific, surprising claim — not a topic summary. TRUE if every finding headline could only describe ONE episode.
2. **findings_are_concise**: Each finding is 2-3 paragraphs max, not mini-essays. TRUE if no finding exceeds 150 words.
3. **deep_analysis_present**: At least 3 findings include an NGM DEEP ANALYSIS callout that surfaces **specific cross-domain mechanistic connections** from the KB. TRUE only if EVERY callout meets ALL of these:
   - **Names specific pathways, molecules, or biomarkers** (e.g., "NAD+→SIRT1→PGC-1α", "DPP-4", "Piezo1/2") — never vague references to "signaling" or "pathways"
   - **Links two or more domains that the source material does not explicitly connect** (e.g., linking senescent cell DPP-4 upregulation to GLP-1 degradation and sugar craving circuits)
   - **Adds mechanistic depth beyond what the finding already states** — not a restatement, clinical translation, or generic practitioner advice
   - FAILS if any callout reads like "For practitioners: treat X as Y" or "Consider monitoring Z" without naming the specific molecular chain that justifies the recommendation
4. **no_forced_themes**: Findings are organized by individual insight, NOT grouped into artificial thematic buckets. TRUE if there are no "Theme 1, Theme 2, Theme 3" headers.

### Writing Quality (3 points)
5. **not_recap_voice**: The writing argues and analyzes, not recaps. TRUE if no finding reads like "In this episode, [podcaster] discussed..."
6. **concrete_specifics**: Every finding includes specific names, numbers, or citations. TRUE if the Ctrl+F test for "many," "several," "significant" returns zero vague hits.
7. **tight_prose**: No paragraph exceeds 3 sentences. No finding uses filler transitions. TRUE if you could not delete a sentence without losing information.

### Structure (3 points)
8. **bottom_line_present**: Executive summary exists and captures the roundup's editorial thesis in 2-3 sentences.
9. **quick_hits_present**: 3-5 one-liner mentions for episodes that don't merit full findings.
10. **what_to_listen_to**: Top 3 episode recommendations with specific reasons.

**Passes when:** At least 8 of 10 criteria are TRUE.

## Lead Magnet Quality Rubric

For long-form educational content with diagrams.

### Content (4 points)
1. **andrew_huberman_voice**: Authoritative, educational, evidence-based — AND approachable. Per `every-voice-patterns.md` → Huberman Accessibility Patterns. Must satisfy all six Huberman rules (short paragraphs, analogy-before-mechanism, why-this-matters signposts, 6–12 one-sentence emphasis beats, 6–10 walk-through phrases, progressive mechanism disclosure). A dense "lecture dump" tone fails this criterion even if the content is accurate.
2. **mechanism_focus**: Explains the "why" behind claims, not just "what"
3. **appropriate_length**: ~1000 words, not padded or truncated
4. **practical_takeaways**: Includes actionable insights, not just theory

### Structure (3 points)
5. **clear_sections**: Logical flow with distinct sections
6. **mechanism_table**: Includes mechanism vs. clinical takeaway table
7. **references_section**: Properly formatted references with citations

### Diagrams (3 points)
8. **diagram_count**: 3-7 diagrams (configurable minimum)
9. **diagram_diversity**: Mix of types (pathway, comparison, process, concept)
10. **diagram_relevance**: Each diagram illustrates a specific concept from the text

### Approachability (6 points — all MANDATORY)

The same six Huberman rules applied to lead magnets. Count carefully — lead magnets are longer than newsletters and tend to accumulate density.

11. **short_paragraphs_lm**: Fewer than 20% of paragraphs exceed 3 sentences. In lead magnets with 5–8 sections, this count must be enforced section-by-section.
12. **analogy_before_mechanism_lm**: Every section introducing an acronym, protein, pathway, or unfamiliar term leads with a plain-English analogy.
13. **signposts_lm**: 5–8 why-this-matters signposts between sections.
14. **one_sentence_beats_lm**: 6–12 one-sentence emphasis paragraphs, never two in a row.
15. **walk_through_phrases_lm**: 6–10 active teaching phrases distributed across sections.
16. **progressive_disclosure_lm**: Every section opens with a hook or analogy, never with an acronym or chemical formula.

**Passes when:** All 16 criteria are TRUE. Approachability failures (11–16) require rewriting the affected sections; they are not negotiable.

## Diagram Validation Criteria

For SVG diagrams embedded in content.

### Technical Validity (4 points)
1. **valid_viewbox**: viewBox is properly defined (e.g., "0 0 600 450")
2. **overflow_visible**: `overflow="visible"` set to prevent clipping
3. **background_set**: Background rect with a warm paper fill — `#FEFDFB` (preferred for Hub Standard) or `#F5F2EC` (legacy acceptable for inline mechanism diagrams)
4. **explicit_strokes**: All shapes have `stroke="none"` unless border intentional

### Text Containment (3 points)
5. **text_within_bounds**: All text positioned at least 40px from viewBox edges
6. **container_sizing**: Containers large enough for their text (text height + 80px padding)
7. **no_overflow**: No text extends beyond its container boundaries

### Visual Quality (3 points)
8. **color_palette**: Uses only NGM palette colors from either (a) the NGM editorial palette in `ngm-style-guide.md` — `#FEFDFB`, `#F5F3EE`, `#1A1A1A`, `#3A3A3A`, `#6A6A6A`, `#8B7355`, `#4A7A5A`, `#D4D0C8` — PREFERRED for lead magnet hero diagrams and any Hub Standard diagram; or (b) the legacy palette in `diagram-guidelines.md` for inline mechanism diagrams.
9. **contrast_adequate**: Text has 4.5:1 minimum contrast ratio
10. **gold_accent_limited**: Accent tones (`#C49A6C` or `#8B7355`) are ≤10% of visual area

**Passes when:** All 10 criteria are TRUE. If any fail, regenerate the diagram with specific fixes.

### Diagram Style Standard Criteria (UNIVERSAL — Apply to Every Diagram Regardless of Layout)

These criteria codify the *style* qualities that made the garlic diagram (`content/graphics/garlic-linkedin/garlic-longevity-hub.html`) feel high-quality. They apply to every lead-magnet diagram, every newsletter diagram, every LinkedIn/social graphic — hub, comparison, cascade, timeline, metaphor scene, or any other layout. Variability in layout is expected and desired. Variability in style is not.

Reference: `diagram-guidelines.md` → "Diagram Style Standard (UNIVERSAL)".

11. **ngm_editorial_palette**: Uses the NGM editorial palette — `#FEFDFB` (paper), `#F5F3EE` (paper-warm), `#1A1A1A` / `#3A3A3A` / `#6A6A6A` / `#9A9A9A` (ink scale), `#D4D0C8` (rule), `#8B7355` (accent brown), `#4A7A5A` (green), `#4A6A7A` (blue), `#B06840` (orange), `#6A5A7A` (purple) — for every lead-magnet diagram and every standalone/social graphic. The legacy `diagram-guidelines.md` palette is acceptable only for small inline mechanism figures. Background is a radial gradient from `#FEFDFB` center to `#F5F3EE` edge via `<radialGradient id="bg">`, not a flat fill.

12. **ngm_editorial_typography**: Uses the three-font editorial system — Cormorant Garamond for titles/deck/subject names, DM Sans for kickers/labels/captions, Source Serif 4 for any body text. Kickers are uppercase DM Sans 11–14px, weight 700, letter-spacing 2–4, accent tone. Titles are Cormorant Garamond 36–52px for hero diagrams, 22–32px inline. Labels on shapes are DM Sans 11–13px. Fails if sans-serif is used for display text or if the font families deviate from this three-font system.

13. **drawn_stylized_subjects**: When the diagram references a concrete object, organism, organ, molecule, or intervention, that subject is rendered as a recognizable silhouette via SVG paths with radial-gradient fill and 2–3 internal highlight strokes — NOT a labeled circle or generic rounded rectangle. See the Drawn-Subject Catalog in `diagram-guidelines.md`. Inline sub-figures may waive this if a drawn subject would eat visual budget, but hero diagrams and LinkedIn graphics must pass. Abstract concepts (a "state," an "equilibrium," a "network") may legitimately use stylized shapes; concrete referents may not.

14. **plain_english_label_hierarchy**: Every label that names a biological effect, pathway, or outcome uses the two-tier voice — a small mechanistic kicker (DM Sans 11px, weight 700, letter-spacing 2, semantic color) and a larger plain-English benefit/effect headline (Cormorant Garamond 18–24px, weight 500, ink color). The headline is always larger and more prominent than the kicker. Optional subtitle in DM Sans 11–12px (ink-3) names specifics. This applies in hub spokes, comparison panels, cascade steps, metaphor-scene captions, and timeline beats. Fails if any label uses acronym-only mechanism text with no plain-English translation.

15. **polished_rendering**: The diagram uses the three cheap-but-nontrivial polish cues that separate NGM style from generic SmartArt: (a) radial gradients on subjects and the background (not flat fills), (b) soft accent-tinted halos behind focal elements at 0.12–0.18 opacity, (c) rhythmic spacing and alignment with 40px+ edge padding and 15–20px internal container padding. Flat/diagrammatic rendering without these cues fails.

16. **editorial_framing**: Hero diagrams (lead-magnet hero, LinkedIn image, slide title card) carry a three-line header (kicker + Cormorant title + italic deck with a thin centered rule) AND a footer banner (left: unifying-insight kicker + explainer + source citation with DOI and effect size; right: NGM wordmark + URL + author byline). Inline sub-figures inside a lead magnet may skip the header but must still carry a thin rule + italic caption + NGM wordmark at the bottom. Standalone/social graphics must have the full header+footer.

17. **standalone_readability**: The diagram — if posted to LinkedIn alone with no caption and no article text — lets a clinician understand (a) the subject, (b) what's happening in the visual, and (c) the unifying claim. Fails if the diagram requires the surrounding article to be intelligible.

**Passes when:** All 7 Style Standard criteria (11–17) are TRUE for every lead-magnet diagram, newsletter diagram, and standalone/social graphic. Failures require regeneration. Small inline mechanism figures inside a lead magnet may relax criterion 11 (legacy palette acceptable), criterion 13 (abstract shapes acceptable), and criterion 16 (header optional, lightweight footer only). The other criteria still apply.

### Layout Selection (Choose the Layout That Fits the Content)

The Style Standard travels across layouts. The layout itself is chosen from the catalog in `diagram-guidelines.md` → "Diagram Types" and "Visual Metaphor Library":

| Content shape | Layout |
|---|---|
| One central concept + 3–10 roughly-parallel downstream outcomes, shared valence | **Hub Layout** |
| Sequential steps or causal chain | **Process Flow / Mechanism Pathway** |
| Before / after, A / B, scalpel vs rewrite, this-drug vs that-drug | **Comparison panel** |
| Time progression, trial phases, historical arc | **Timeline** |
| Nested hierarchy or taxonomy | **Concept/Hierarchy** |
| Two-variable positioning | **Quadrant/Positioning Chart** |
| Physical-world scene that teaches a mechanism (tinderbox, pac-man pathogen, parking brake) | **Visual Metaphor** |

Each layout has its own structural conventions (see `diagram-guidelines.md`). The Style Standard applies to all of them.

### Hub Layout Criteria (Additional — Only When Hub Layout Is Chosen)

These are *layout-specific* rules that apply only to diagrams using the Hub Layout. They are in addition to the universal Style Standard, not a replacement for it.

H1. **hub_layout_fits_content**: The content genuinely has radial structure — one central concept with 3–10 downstream outcomes that share a valence (all beneficial, all risks, or all markers) and have no meaningful order among themselves. Fails if the "spokes" have sequential order, causal dependency, or mixed valence — use a different layout instead.

H2. **radial_node_placement**: Nodes are placed on a clock face around the center. 8 nodes at 12, 1:30, 3, 4:30, 6, 7:30, 9, 10:30. 6 nodes at 12, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10. 5 nodes at 72° spacing.

H3. **dashed_ring_guides**: Two faint dashed concentric rings at roughly 270px and 370px radius (on a 1200×1200 canvas), `stroke="#D4D0C8"`, `stroke-dasharray="2,6"`, `opacity="0.5"`.

H4. **consistent_spoke_valence**: All connector lines, spoke-dots, and spoke-card borders use one valence color across all spokes — green (`#4A7A5A`) if all outcomes are beneficial, orange (`#B06840`) if all are cautions, blue (`#4A6A7A`) if all are neutral/informational. Fails if spokes mix colors — that signals the content shouldn't be a hub.

H5. **central_subject_halo**: The drawn subject at the center has a soft radial-gradient halo behind it (`<radialGradient id="glow">` with accent stop at 0.18 opacity) that makes the focal anchor visually magnetic.

**Passes when:** Hub Layout diagrams satisfy all 5 hub-specific criteria (H1–H5) in addition to the Style Standard. For any other layout, these rules do NOT apply — use the corresponding layout's conventions instead.

## How to Use These Rubrics

When self-critiquing:
1. Read through your content
2. Evaluate each criterion honestly
3. For failures, note the specific issue and location
4. Revise to address failures
5. Re-evaluate until all criteria pass

The goal is not perfection on first draft—it's iteration until quality threshold is met.
